# Korean-native copy

Use this reference when Korean public copy appears in service-vernacular work. Korean copy must pass Korean-native review by meaning and reader intent, not sentence-by-sentence translation from English.

## Korean-native review

Korean-native review asks whether a Korean reader would understand the same intent with natural rhythm, trust, and register. It is not a literal parity check. Preserve the claim boundary and protected terms, then rewrite the surrounding Korean so it reads as Korean-first public copy.

Required review steps:

1. Identify the reader, surface, and copy job from the intent card.
2. Restate the meaning in Korean before comparing words to the English source.
3. Check whether the Korean sentence order follows Korean information flow, not English word order.
4. Confirm protected English product names remain unchanged unless `LANGUAGE.md` says otherwise.
5. Adjust particles, spacing, and surrounding grammar so protected English terms sit naturally in Korean.
6. Read the line aloud for headline rhythm, breath, register, and trust.

## Translationese and sentence shape

Reject translationese when Korean keeps English word order, argument order, modifier chains, or abstract nouns that Korean readers would not naturally use.

Watch for these failure shapes:

1. English word order with the Korean verb delayed after too many imported modifiers.
2. `noun stacks` that pack product, category, proof, and action into one long noun phrase.
3. Comma-separated verb chains that mirror English feature lists instead of Korean clause rhythm.
4. Awkward collocations where the noun and verb do not normally pair in Korean.
5. Mixed register, such as technical trust copy shifting between casual marketing tone and stiff institutional language.
6. Over-honorifics that make a product claim sound like customer-service script copy.
7. Headlines that are grammatically valid but hard to say aloud.

## Protected English terms

Protected English product names, brand names, API names, SDK names, model names, feature names, and contract terms remain unchanged unless `LANGUAGE.md` says otherwise.

Keeping the English term is not enough. Korean particles around English terms must still be natural. Choose particles by sound and sentence role, and rewrite the surrounding Korean if the English noun placement creates a stiff or ambiguous line.

Examples of checks:

1. Does the English noun need a Korean object, topic, or subject particle to make the role clear?
2. Does the sentence sound better if the English product name moves closer to the verb?
3. Does the protected noun force a noun stack that should become a clause?
4. Does the Korean sentence over-explain the English product name in public copy?

## Register for B2B technical Korean

B2B technical Korean can require more restraint than consumer-product Korean. Calm `합니다체` may be better than consumer-product `해요체` for trust-sensitive homepage/service prose, especially when the page sells infrastructure, safety, compliance, robotics, AI operations, security, or other high-risk work.

Use `해요체` only when the surface, brand, audience, and evidence support a warmer conversational tone. Do not switch registers inside one copy block unless the surface has a clear reason.

## Headline rhythm and read-aloud checks

Korean homepage and service headlines need rhythm, not just accurate meaning. A line can fail when it is correct but heavy.

Run these checks before accepting Korean public copy:

1. Read the headline aloud once at normal speed.
2. If the breath breaks in the middle of a noun stack, rewrite the stack into a clearer clause.
3. If the verb feels imported from English, choose a Korean collocation that matches the claim.
4. If the sentence needs commas to survive, split or reorder it.
5. If the tone sounds like a translated slide deck, rewrite from the Korean reader's decision moment.

## Negative fixtures

Mark Korean failures as `failed public copy / do not publish` when they show translationese, English word order, awkward protected English noun placement, unnatural Korean particles around English terms, noun stacks, comma-separated verb chains, awkward collocations, mixed register, over-honorifics, or failed read-aloud checks.

Negative fixtures are evidence for rejection only. They are not final Korean copy and must not be softened into an accepted recommendation without a fresh Korean-native review.
