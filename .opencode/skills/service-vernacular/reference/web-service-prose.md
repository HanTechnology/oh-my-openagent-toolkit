# Web/service prose

Use this reference for homepage/service prose: public copy on a homepage, product page, service page, landing page, pricing-adjacent page, metadata surface, or public fallback state. These surfaces sell trust, fit, and next action. They are not the place to expose page plans, QA notes, or internal proof workflows.

## Homepage/service prose surfaces

| Surface | Copy job | Evidence density | Acceptable register | Rejection patterns |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `hero` | State the reader's main problem, desired outcome, or category fit fast. | Low to medium: one grounded claim or domain noun is enough. | Clear, confident, specific. | Vague transformation claims, internal page setup, inflated category invention, or slogans that could fit any SaaS app. |
| subhead | Explain who it is for, what it helps them do, and why it matters now. | Medium: name the audience, workflow, and outcome. | Plain, buyer-aware, and concrete. | Feature piles, noun stacks, route sequencing, or explanation of the hero's purpose. |
| `proof panel` | Make trust legible through evidence the reader can judge. | High: cite concrete capability, workflow proof, integration, artifact, metric, or reviewable fact. | Calm and verifiable. | Talking about proof layers, QA coverage, claim checks, or evidence collection instead of the proven public fact. |
| `route card` | Help the reader choose the right product, service, audience path, or next page. | Medium: name the choice, fit, and outcome. | Scannable and decision-oriented. | Visitor-routing explanations, page-order narration, or labels that require internal taxonomy knowledge. |
| product/service section | Show what the product or service does for the buyer's job. | Medium to high: connect capability, workflow, and result. | Specific, useful, and restrained. | Feature inventories without a reader job, architecture-first copy, or claims beyond evidence. |
| `CTA` | Tell the reader what happens next. | Low: one clear action and expected result. | Direct, predictable, and action-ready. | Clever labels, vague verbs like `unlock`, hidden commitments, or unclear destination. |
| navigation label | Let readers find the right surface quickly. | Low: use the recognized noun. | Short and conventional. | Branded labels for standard destinations, duplicated terms, or labels that hide the page purpose. |
| caption | Explain the nearby artifact or proof without repeating the heading. | Medium: name the artifact and why it matters. | Concise and factual. | Decorative captions, QA commentary, or implementation notes. |
| metadata | Summarize the page for search, sharing, previews, or assistive contexts. | Medium: include the main audience, object, and outcome. | Accurate and compact. | Keyword stuffing, unsupported claims, or internal planning language. |
| public fallback text | Keep trust when content, service, or data is unavailable. | Medium: name the state and safe next step. | Calm and recovery-oriented. | Blame, vague apologies, hidden system details, or missing next action. |

## Buyer and job-to-be-done framing

For homepage/service prose, name the buying or evaluation context before polishing style:

1. Audience: who is reading and what they already understand.
2. Decision moment: why they are on this page now.
3. Problem: what risk, delay, confusion, cost, or missed outcome they want to solve.
4. Outcome: what changes for them if the product or service fits.
5. Proof: what evidence lets them believe the claim.
6. Difference: what makes this product or service distinct without inventing a broad category promise.

If those six points cannot be filled from evidence, weaken the claim or mark the draft provisional.

## CTA next-action rules

Every CTA or link must make the next action predictable:

1. The reader should know whether they will book, buy, read, compare, configure, download, contact, or start a workflow.
2. The label should match the destination or action, not the writer's desired emotion.
3. The surrounding copy should disclose meaningful commitment, such as sales contact, account creation, download, or external navigation.
4. Keep standard labels when standard labels are clearer than branded verbs.

Reject CTA copy when the reader cannot predict what happens after selection.

## Locale-native hero and CTA transcreation

Localized homepage heroes and CTAs often need transcreation, not sentence-by-sentence translation. Preserve source intent, claim boundary, destination, and commitment level, then let the target locale choose its own order, rhythm, proof emphasis, idiom choice, and CTA verb.

Record the transcreation decision, termbase choices, protected terms, UI/layout risk, and native-review evidence in notes. The visible hero or CTA must not say that it was localized, transcreated, or native-reviewed.

Reject localized hero or CTA copy when it copies source syntax at the cost of native reading, changes the offer, overstates proof, hides the link destination, ignores text expansion, or turns translation rationale into public copy.

## Evidence density

Use low evidence density for short labels, navigation, and obvious actions. Use medium evidence density for subheads, route cards, captions, and metadata. Use high evidence density for proof panels, product/service claims, and trust-sensitive fallback text.

Density is not length. A short claim can be dense when it carries the right domain noun, audience, proof, or consequence.

## Public fallback text

Fallback text is public copy. It should state the visible state, preserve trust, and give a safe next step when one exists. Do not expose stack details, implementation routes, QA status, or internal incident labels unless the audience is an operator surface where those terms are already part of the public contract.
